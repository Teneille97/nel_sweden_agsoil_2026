Soil organic matter decomposition rates at agricultural sites in Sweden

Author

Teneille Nel

Teneille’s project repository is available on Github at this link.

Overview

Four agricultural sites were established in Sweden (M2, M4, M6 and R94) and soils were samples from 1957 - 2019. Bulk soil samples were fractionated by thermal combustion, isolating two pools (325-C and 400-C stable). Carbon content and radiocarbon signatures of the samples were measured, mean soil bulk density was determined plant-derived carbon inputs were estimated.

Simple two-pool models

We fit two-pool models (script available here) using observed C stocks and \(^{14}\)C measurements of bulk soil and organic matter fractions, where fractions stable at 325 and 400 degrees are treated as the “fast” and slow-cycling pools, respectively.

Key points

  • Because the 325-stable pool has technically already lost the faster-cycling pool that was initially combusted, we see over-predictions of the fractions to compensate for this “missing carbon”.
  • The observed \(^{14}\)C of bulk soil remains constant despite the predicted increase that was predicted during the atmospheric spike in bomb carbon; this means that very little new carbon was incorporated into soil organic matter, potentially due to erosion and/ or management practices such as tillage or residue removals.

Modeling the invisible intermediate pool

We fit two-pool models by ignoring the bulk measurements and creating a system where the 325-stable pool is seen as as “bulk” soil and the 400-stable soil remains the slow pool. In this scenario, the observed ‘fast’ pool carbon stocks are calculated as the difference between 325-stable and 400-stable carbon stocks, while the fast pool \(^{14}\)C values are strictly modeled.

Key points

  • The models show that the observed carbon stocks and \(^{14}\)C values of the 325-stable pool are well explained by a heterogeneous system of a faster cycling pool along with the residual pool.
  • This means that our 325 pool, although modeled initially as a single fast-cycling pool, actually represents a nested pool of intermediate- and slow-cycling carbon.
  • Preliminary attempts to model the intermediate fraction in a 3-pool nested system did not give good fits.

Modeling the invisible fast pool

We fit two-pool models with the actual bulk pool and treating the 325-stable pool, as the “slow” pool, leaving out the 400-stable pool. In this nested scenario, the observed fast pool carbon stocks are calculated as the difference between bulk and 325-stable carbon stocks, while the fast pool \(^{14}\)C values are strictly modeled.

Key points

  • The models show that the observed carbon stocks and \(^{14}\)C values of the bulk soil are well explained by a heterogeneous system of a fast-cycling pool along with the 325-stable pool.
  • This means that combustion at 325 degrees removed a fast-cycling fraction which was not captured in our initial models, which treat the 325-pool as a fast fraction, even though it is technically a nested pool of intermediate- and slow-cycling carbon.

Another nested model attempt

In this scenario, the observed intermediate pool carbon stocks are calculated as the difference between 325-stable carbon and 400-stable carbon stocks, while the intermediate pool \(^{14}\)C values are strictly modeled.

Key points

  • The \(^{14}\)C trends of intermediate pool differed a lot between treatments, potentially compensating for bad fits
  • The intermediate pool, although small, seems to play an important role in explaining the observed bulk \(^{14}\)C values
  • In some treatments, the pool is highly stable, but in one treatment it incorporates an unreasonable amount of modern carbon from the fast pool - perhaps the systems collapses from an initial 3-pool system to a 2-pool system due to changes management at some point in time?
